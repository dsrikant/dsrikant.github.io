# Trading With Claude: My First $20 Experiment on the Wildest Market Day of 2026

_Dushyant Remivasan | March 9, 2026_

---

I've been curious about AI for a while — not just as a tech trend, but as a practical tool for everyday decisions. So I started an experiment: what happens if I use Claude, Anthropic's AI assistant, to help me learn how to trade? Not with fake money, not in a simulator — but with real dollars, in real time, on a real brokerage.

Today was my first live session. And I picked one hell of a day to start.

---

## The Setup

I have $25 in a Robinhood account. My goal wasn't to get rich. It was simple: can Claude help me understand what's happening in the market, make a coherent plan, and walk me through a trade — all in plain English?

I told Claude what I had to work with and asked it to review the day's market conditions and suggest a play.

---

## What Was Happening in the Market Today

March 9, 2026 was not a normal Monday. Over the weekend, the U.S. and Israel launched military strikes on Iran, triggering a closure of the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most critical oil shipping routes. By Sunday night, crude oil had surged past $100 a barrel for the first time in years. Stock futures were in freefall.

When the market opened Monday morning, the Dow dropped nearly 900 points at its low. Almost every sector was bleeding red. Banks were down 3%+. Boeing, Cisco, big tech — all taking hits.

But there was one pocket of green: **semiconductors**.

Broadcom was up over 3%, AMD up nearly 2%, Micron up over 2%. Claude flagged this immediately — chips were the one sector holding up, likely because the Iran conflict doesn't directly threaten semiconductor supply chains the way it threatens oil-dependent industries.

---

## The Plan Claude Suggested

Given my $25 budget, Claude walked me through the logic of using **SOXL** — the Direxion Daily Semiconductor Bull 3x ETF. It's a leveraged ETF that moves at roughly 3x the daily performance of semiconductor stocks. Since Robinhood supports fractional shares, I could put my full position in even though SOXL trades above $25 per share.

The thesis was straightforward:

- Chips are the only sector with real momentum today
- Oil spiking above $100 was the primary fear driver — if oil pulled back, chips would rally further
- SOXL amplifies that move, giving a small account real upside potential

The realistic target wasn't to get rich. Claude recalibrated my expectations from an aggressive "$10 profit" goal down to a more honest **$3–5 target** — about a 12–20% return on the $20 I planned to deploy.

---

## What I Actually Did

I invested **$20 at $49.73 per share** — picking up 0.402183 fractional shares of SOXL.

Almost immediately, the position went against me. SOXL dropped to around $44–$45. I was down roughly $2, sitting at about -10%. Not a great feeling.

But then the macro picture shifted. Crude oil — which had briefly spiked above $100 at the open — started falling. By midday it had dropped to $82 a barrel. That's a massive single-session reversal, and it changed everything for the chip trade.

I checked back in with Claude, gave it the updated oil price and my current position, and asked: do we stay in or get out?

Claude's read: **hold**. The oil drop was exactly the catalyst the thesis needed. Chips should recover as inflation fears ease. The stop-loss was set at $51 — if SOXL dropped back there, exit to protect the small gain that was starting to build.

---

## One Thing I Learned: Fractional Shares Have Limits

I asked Claude how to set a limit sell order so I wouldn't have to watch the screen all afternoon. It told me something I didn't know: **Robinhood doesn't support limit orders on fractional shares.** You can only do market sells.

So instead, Claude walked me through setting a price alert in the app — the bell icon on the stock page — so I'd get notified when SOXL hit my target, and then manually execute a market sell. Not perfect, but it works.

---

## The Result

I watched SOXL climb through the afternoon as oil continued to ease and the broader market recovered off its lows. When it hit **$53.31**, I executed the market sell.

**Final numbers:**

- Bought: 0.402183 shares @ $49.73 → $20.00 invested
- Sold: 0.402183 shares @ $53.31 → $21.44 received
- **Profit: +$1.44 (+7.2%) in a single session**

My total account went from $25.00 to $26.44.

---

## What I Actually Learned

A $1.44 profit isn't going to change my life. But that's not the point of this experiment. Here's what I took away from today:

**1. Context matters more than picks.** Claude didn't just give me a ticker. It explained _why_ semiconductors were the right sector given the macro environment — oil shock, geopolitical fear, where money was rotating. Understanding the "why" made it easier to hold through the dip instead of panic selling.

**2. Adjusting the plan in real time is the skill.** When I came back with the updated oil price and my underwater position, Claude didn't stick rigidly to the original plan. It looked at the new information and gave me a revised read. That kind of dynamic adjustment is something I want to get better at.

**3. Knowing the platform matters.** The fractional share / limit order limitation was a real gap in my knowledge. Small things like that can cost you money if you don't know them going in.

**4. Starting small is the right move.** A $20 trade on a $25 account on a day this volatile could have gone sideways fast. The discipline to size down, take a realistic profit target, and not get greedy — Claude helped me stay anchored to that.

If you're curious about the intersection of AI and personal finance, follow along. I'll post after every session — wins, losses, and everything in between.
